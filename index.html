About Eiga

Welcome to Eiga (えいが). — a curated tribute to the craft of cinema: a space to explore remarkable stories, visionary directors, and unforgettable performances.

▶ Why Cinema Matters

Film is more than entertainment — it is a mirror held up to the human experience. Great movies change how we see ourselves and the world. Eiga celebrates that power.

Cinema spans continents and cultures. From the golden age of Hollywood to the bold new wave movements of Europe and Asia, every era has produced films that define the human experience. Eiga celebrates them all.

★ Did You Know?

The first public film screening was held by the Lumière brothers in Paris on December 28, 1895 — making cinema over 130 years old!

Film Glossary

Auteur
A director with a personal style so strong they are the film's primary author.
B-Roll
Supplementary footage intercut with the main shot to provide context or visual variety.
Chiaroscuro
The dramatic use of light and shadow, borrowed from painting, to create depth and mood.
Diegetic Sound
Sound that exists within the world of the film — dialogue, footsteps, a radio playing on screen.
Dutch Angle
A tilted camera shot used to suggest unease, disorientation, or psychological tension.
Jump Cut
An abrupt edit between two similar shots that creates a disjointed sense of time passing.
Leitmotif
A recurring musical theme associated with a character, place, or idea throughout the film.
MacGuffin
A plot device that motivates characters but is largely irrelevant to the story.
Match Cut
A transition where a shape or movement in one shot is continued by a similar shape in the next.
Mise-en-scène
Everything visible in a frame — set design, lighting, and actor placement.
Montage
An editing technique that juxtaposes a series of shots to condense time or create an idea.

Explore Genres

▶ Science Fiction

Sci-Fi pushes the boundaries of imagination. From dystopian futures to interstellar exploration, these films ask the deepest questions about humanity and technology. Masterpieces of the genre include Blade Runner, Interstellar, and Arrival.

▶ Drama

Drama is the backbone of cinema. Character-driven narratives exploring love, loss, ambition, and redemption have produced some of the most emotionally powerful experiences in film history.

▶ Thriller & Suspense

Tension, dread, and the unknown. Thriller films keep audiences on the edge of their seats — from psychological mind-games to high-stakes action. Hitchcock defined the genre; countless directors have built upon it since.

▶ Animation

Animation transcends the limits of live-action to build entirely new worlds. From the Studio Ghibli universe of wonder to Pixar's emotionally resonant stories, animated films are far more than children's entertainment — they are profound works of art.

▶ Romance

Romance films explore the electric, tender, and often painful landscape of love. From sweeping epics to intimate conversations over a single night, the best romantic films — like Before Sunrise or Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind — feel intensely personal.

▶ Horror

Horror confronts our deepest primal fears. Whether it uses supernatural dread, slasher tension, or slow psychological unraveling, the genre has produced some of the most technically creative films ever made — from The Shining to Hereditary.

▶ Documentary

Documentary cinema transforms reality into narrative. Filmmakers like Werner Herzog and Errol Morris have elevated the form into high art — revealing the strange, beautiful, and troubling truths lurking beneath the surface of everyday life.

▶ Comedy

Comedy is deceptively difficult. The best comedies — from screwball classics to dry deadpan — demand extraordinary timing, sharp writing, and fearless performance. Films like Booksmart and Bottoms prove the genre is richer than ever.

▶ Historical & Period Films

Period films transport us across time with meticulous costume, architecture, and atmosphere. Whether revisiting wartime Europe or Victorian England, the best historical dramas find timeless human truths within their specific eras.

▶ Music & Musical

Music films tap into emotion on a frequency that dialogue alone cannot reach. From jukebox musicals like Bohemian Rhapsody to original sung-through narratives like La La Land, these films use song and sound as the primary language of the soul.
